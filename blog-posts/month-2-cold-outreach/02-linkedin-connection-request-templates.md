# 15 LinkedIn Connection Request Templates That Get Accepted

Your LinkedIn connection request is the front door to every B2B relationship on the platform. Get it wrong, and that door stays shut. Get it right, and you have opened a direct line to decision-makers who can transform your pipeline.

The problem is that most connection requests are terrible. Generic messages like "I would like to add you to my professional network" or "Let us connect and explore synergies" are instantly ignored by busy professionals who receive dozens of these each week.

In this post, we share 15 connection request templates organized by scenario, along with the personalization principles that make each one work. These templates are designed for B2B professionals in SaaS, consulting, recruitment, finance, and marketing -- but the underlying principles apply to any industry.

## Why Your Connection Requests Get Ignored

Before we dive into templates, let us understand why most requests fail.

**They are generic.** If your message could be sent to anyone, it will resonate with no one. Prospects can spot a copy-paste message instantly.

**They lead with a pitch.** Connection requests that immediately ask for a meeting or pitch a service trigger the same reaction as a stranger on the street trying to sell you something. The timing is wrong.

**They provide no context.** The recipient has no idea why you want to connect or what value the connection offers them.

**They are too long.** LinkedIn limits connection request notes to 300 characters. Even within that limit, shorter messages tend to perform better because they respect the prospect's time.

## Personalization Principles That Matter

Every effective connection request follows three principles.

**Relevance.** Explain why you are reaching out to this specific person. Reference something concrete -- their role, company, content, or a shared connection.

**Brevity.** Say what you need to say in as few words as possible. The 300-character limit forces discipline, but even within that limit, aim for concise.

**Value orientation.** Frame the connection in terms of what the recipient gains, not what you want from them.

Now, let us get into the templates.

## Templates for Cold Prospects

These templates are for reaching out to people you have no prior relationship with.

### Template 1: The Industry Peer

*Best for: Connecting with someone in your industry or a complementary role.*

> Hi [First Name], I work in [industry/function] and have been following the work [Company] is doing in [specific area]. Would be great to connect and exchange ideas on [relevant topic].

**Why it works:** It positions you as a peer, not a seller. Mentioning their company and a specific area of work shows you have done your homework.

### Template 2: The Content Appreciator

*Best for: Connecting with someone who actively posts on LinkedIn.*

> [First Name], your recent post on [topic] really resonated -- especially the point about [specific detail]. I write about similar topics and would love to connect.

**Why it works:** Referencing a specific post proves you are not mass-messaging. People who create content appreciate thoughtful engagement.

### Template 3: The Shared Challenge

*Best for: Connecting with peers who face similar business challenges.*

> Hi [First Name], I noticed you are leading [function] at [Company]. We are tackling similar challenges around [specific challenge] at [Your Company]. Would be great to connect and compare notes.

**Why it works:** It frames the connection as mutually beneficial. You are not asking for something -- you are offering to share experiences.

### Template 4: The Complimenter

*Best for: Connecting with someone whose company has recently achieved something notable.*

> [First Name], congrats on [Company]'s recent [achievement/milestone/funding round]. Impressive growth. I work with companies in [industry] and would love to connect.

**Why it works:** Leading with genuine recognition is disarming. It shows awareness without being sycophantic.

### Template 5: The Direct Approach

*Best for: Connecting with someone when you have a clear, relevant reason.*

> Hi [First Name], I help [target role/industry] companies solve [specific problem]. Not pitching -- just thought it would be valuable to be in each other's networks. Happy to connect.

**Why it works:** The "not pitching" caveat lowers defenses, and being upfront about what you do builds trust. Use this sparingly and only when your value proposition is tightly relevant.

## Templates for Mutual Connections

When you share connections with a prospect, leverage that social proof.

### Template 6: The Mutual Connection Reference

*Best for: When you share a meaningful connection with the prospect.*

> Hi [First Name], I see we are both connected with [Mutual Connection]. They speak highly of the work at [Company]. Would be great to connect directly.

**Why it works:** Mutual connections serve as implicit endorsements. Mentioning them creates immediate familiarity.

### Template 7: The Shared Group Member

*Best for: When you and the prospect belong to the same LinkedIn group or community.*

> [First Name], I noticed we are both members of [Group Name]. Your perspective on [topic related to group] caught my attention. Would love to connect.

**Why it works:** Shared group membership establishes common ground and shared interests.

### Template 8: The Alumni Connection

*Best for: When you share an educational institution or previous employer.*

> Hi [First Name], fellow [University/Company] alum here. I am now working in [industry/function] and always enjoy connecting with others from [shared institution]. Great to see your work at [Company].

**Why it works:** Shared history creates an instant bond. People are significantly more likely to accept requests from alumni.

## Templates for Event Follow-Ups

If you have attended the same event, conference, or webinar, use that shared experience.

### Template 9: The Conference Follow-Up

*Best for: Connecting after meeting someone briefly at an event.*

> [First Name], great meeting you at [Event Name]. I enjoyed our conversation about [specific topic]. Would love to stay connected and continue the discussion.

**Why it works:** It references a real interaction, which makes the request feel like a natural next step rather than a cold outreach.

### Template 10: The Webinar Attendee

*Best for: Connecting with someone who attended or presented at the same virtual event.*

> Hi [First Name], I caught your presentation at [Webinar/Event] on [topic]. Your take on [specific point] was spot on. Would love to connect.

**Why it works:** Virtual events are networking opportunities that most people fail to capitalize on. This template turns passive attendance into an active connection.

### Template 11: The Same-Speaker Fan

*Best for: When you attended the same session at a conference.*

> [First Name], we were both at [Speaker]'s session at [Event] -- the insights on [topic] were valuable. Always good to connect with others in the space.

**Why it works:** Shared experiences, even passive ones like watching the same presentation, create enough common ground for a connection.

## Templates for Following Up on Engagement

These templates are for people who have already interacted with you in some way.

### Template 12: The Post Commenter

*Best for: When someone has commented on your LinkedIn post.*

> [First Name], thanks for your comment on my post about [topic]. You raised a great point about [their specific comment]. Would love to connect and keep the conversation going.

**Why it works:** They have already engaged with you, so the connection request feels natural. Referencing their specific comment adds personalization.

### Template 13: The Profile Viewer

*Best for: When LinkedIn shows someone has viewed your profile.*

> Hi [First Name], I noticed you checked out my profile. I am working in [industry/function] and it looks like we have some overlap. Happy to connect.

**Why it works:** Profile views indicate existing curiosity. This template capitalizes on that interest while it is still fresh.

### Template 14: The Content Sharer

*Best for: When someone has shared your content or a piece of content you both found valuable.*

> [First Name], saw you shared [article/post] about [topic]. Great taste -- that piece changed how I think about [related point]. Would be great to connect.

**Why it works:** Shared content interests signal intellectual alignment, which is a strong foundation for a professional connection.

## Template for Reconnecting

### Template 15: The Dormant Connection Re-Engagement

*Best for: When you want to re-engage with someone you have lost touch with.*

> Hi [First Name], it has been a while since we connected. I see you have moved into [new role/company] -- congratulations. Would love to catch up on what you are working on.

**Why it works:** Acknowledging the gap honestly while showing awareness of their career progression demonstrates genuine interest.

## How to Personalize These Templates Effectively

Templates are starting points, not scripts. Here is how to make each one genuinely personal.

### Research Before You Reach Out

Spend 60 to 90 seconds on each prospect's profile before sending a request. Look for recent posts or articles they have shared, career transitions or promotions, company news or announcements, shared interests or experiences, and mutual connections worth mentioning.

This small time investment dramatically improves your acceptance rate.

### Match Your Tone to Your Prospect

A connection request to a startup founder should feel different from one to a corporate VP. Adapt your language and formality level to match your prospect's communication style, which you can gauge from their posts and profile.

### Avoid These Common Mistakes

**Do not use the prospect's full name.** "Hi John" feels natural. "Hi John Michael Smith" feels robotic.

**Do not include links.** URLs in connection requests often trigger spam filters and reduce acceptance rates.

**Do not ask for anything.** The connection request is step one. Save the ask for after they accept.

**Do not use buzzwords.** Words like "synergy," "leverage," and "disruptive" make people's eyes glaze over. Write like a human being.

### A Note on Volume vs. Quality

You might be tempted to send the maximum number of connection requests each week using generic messages. Resist that temptation. LinkedIn's algorithm tracks your acceptance rate. If too many people ignore or mark your requests as spam, LinkedIn will restrict your account.

A better approach is to send fewer, highly personalized requests. An 80% acceptance rate on 50 weekly requests will build your network faster than a 20% acceptance rate on 100 requests -- and it will keep your account in good standing.

## What Happens After They Accept

Getting the connection accepted is just the beginning. Here is what to do next.

**Wait 24 hours.** Do not immediately hit them with a pitch. Let the connection breathe.

**Send a thank-you message.** A brief message thanking them for connecting and offering something of value sets the right tone.

**Engage with their content.** Like and comment on their posts before sending any business-related messages. This builds familiarity and warms up the relationship.

**Start a conversation, not a pitch.** When you do message them, lead with a question or observation about their business, not a product demo request.
