# How to Use Video in Your Cold Outreach Strategy (With Examples)

Cold outreach is a numbers game -- but it does not have to be a low-quality one. The biggest challenge in B2B prospecting is not finding email addresses or building contact lists. It is getting a response from people who are drowning in generic messages from strangers.

Video changes the dynamic. Instead of another forgettable text block, your prospect sees a face, hears a voice, and watches someone speak directly to them. The result is outreach that feels personal, memorable, and worthy of a reply.

This guide walks you through exactly how to incorporate video into your cold outreach strategy -- the types of videos that work, how to record and edit them efficiently, how to embed them in emails and LinkedIn messages, and real examples you can adapt for your own campaigns.

## Why Video Outreach Gets Better Response Rates

### It Humanizes the Interaction

Text-based cold outreach feels transactional. Video outreach feels like a conversation. When a prospect sees you speaking naturally, referencing their company, and offering genuine value, the psychological barrier to responding drops significantly.

### It Demonstrates Effort and Credibility

Anyone can send a mass email. Recording a video -- even a short one -- demonstrates that you invested time specifically for this prospect. That investment signals seriousness and respect, both of which increase the likelihood of engagement.

### It Communicates More in Less Time

A 60-second video can convey more information, context, and personality than a 500-word email. Video combines visual, auditory, and emotional cues that text alone cannot deliver. Complex ideas become clearer, and your enthusiasm and expertise come through naturally.

## Types of Videos for Cold Outreach

Different stages of the outreach process call for different types of video. Here are the four core video types you should have in your arsenal.

### The Introduction Video

**Purpose:** Make a memorable first impression and establish relevance.

**Length:** 45-75 seconds.

**Content:** Introduce yourself, explain why you are reaching out, and briefly mention how you can help. Reference something specific about the prospect's company or role to show you have done your research.

**Example script outline:**

"Hi [Name], I am [Your Name] from [Company]. I came across your profile while researching [industry/topic] and noticed that [specific observation about their company]. We have been working with companies like [similar company] to [achieve specific outcome]. I thought it would be worth connecting because [bridge to value]. Would you be open to a quick chat next week to explore whether this could be relevant for [their company]?"

### The Screen Share Demo

**Purpose:** Show, do not just tell. Walk the prospect through something relevant using a screen recording.

**Length:** 60-120 seconds.

**Content:** Share your screen showing the prospect's website, LinkedIn profile, or marketing materials. Point out specific observations or opportunities. Transition to showing how your solution addresses what you found.

**When to use it:** This format works exceptionally well for agencies, consultants, and SaaS companies that can visually demonstrate value. For example, a marketing agency might record a quick audit of the prospect's LinkedIn presence, or a SaaS company might show how their product would look with the prospect's data.

### The Testimonial Clip

**Purpose:** Build trust through social proof.

**Length:** 30-60 seconds.

**Content:** A short clip from a satisfied client describing the results they achieved by working with you. This is not a fully produced testimonial video -- it is a conversational snippet that feels authentic and relatable.

**When to use it:** Include in follow-up sequences after the initial outreach. When a prospect has seen your intro video but has not responded, a testimonial clip provides third-party validation that can tip them toward engaging.

### The Follow-Up Video

**Purpose:** Re-engage prospects who did not respond to initial outreach.

**Length:** 30-45 seconds.

**Content:** Acknowledge your previous message without being pushy. Add a new piece of value -- a relevant article, a case study, a new insight about their industry. Keep the tone light and give them an easy way to respond.

**Example script outline:**

"Hey [Name], I sent you a quick video last week about [topic]. Totally understand if the timing was not right. I wanted to share something new -- we just published [resource/case study] about [relevant topic] that I thought you might find interesting. Either way, no pressure. If a conversation would be helpful, I am happy to find a time that works."

## Recording Tips for Professional-Quality Videos

You do not need a studio setup, but small improvements in your recording environment make a meaningful difference in how your videos are perceived.

### Lighting

Face a window or use a ring light positioned directly in front of you. Avoid overhead lighting that creates harsh shadows. Side lighting can work but often creates an uneven look. The goal is soft, even illumination across your face.

### Audio

Use an external microphone if possible. Even an inexpensive lapel microphone dramatically improves audio quality compared to a laptop's built-in microphone. If you must use your laptop mic, record in a quiet room and sit within two feet of the computer.

### Background

Keep your background clean and professional. A bookshelf, a plain wall, or a tidy office space all work well. If your background is distracting, use a virtual background -- but choose one that looks natural and professional, not a stock photo of a beach.

### Camera Positioning

Position your camera at eye level. Looking down at a laptop camera creates an unflattering angle and subconsciously communicates that you are not speaking to the viewer as an equal. Stack your laptop on books or use an external webcam mounted on your monitor.

### Your Delivery

Speak as if you are talking to a colleague, not presenting to an audience. Make eye contact with the camera lens (not the screen). Smile naturally. Use hand gestures if they feel natural. The more conversational you are, the more engaging the video will be.

## Video Editing for Outreach: Keeping It Efficient

Editing is where many people get stuck. They record a video and then spend 20 minutes trying to make it perfect. For cold outreach video, minimal editing is actually an advantage -- it keeps the video feeling authentic rather than overly produced.

### What to Edit

- **Trim the beginning and end.** Cut any awkward pauses before you start speaking and after you finish.
- **Remove major fumbles.** If you stumbled over a sentence badly, cut it. But minor imperfections are fine and can actually make you seem more human.
- **Add a custom thumbnail.** Design a thumbnail in Canva or your design tool of choice that includes the prospect's name or company. This dramatically increases click rates.

### What Not to Edit

- **Do not add heavy transitions or effects.** This is not a marketing video; it is a personal message. Keep it natural.
- **Do not add background music.** Music makes the video feel like a marketing asset rather than a personal conversation.
- **Do not over-polish your delivery.** If you re-record the same video five times to get it perfect, you will sound rehearsed and stiff.

### Batch Editing Workflow

If you are producing multiple outreach videos per day, establish a streamlined workflow:

1. Record all videos in a single session.
2. Import into your editing tool (most video outreach platforms have built-in trimming).
3. Trim the start and end of each video.
4. Add custom thumbnails.
5. Upload and attach to your outreach messages.

This process should take less than two minutes per video once you have practiced it a few times.

## Embedding Video in Emails

Embedding video in emails requires some technical know-how, since most email clients do not support actual video playback.

### The Animated GIF Method

The most common approach is to use an animated GIF or a static image with a play button overlay that links to the hosted video. When the recipient clicks the image, they are taken to a landing page where the video plays.

Most video outreach platforms (Loom, Vidyard, BombBomb) generate these automatically. They create a clickable thumbnail that you can paste directly into your email.

### Best Practices for Email Video

- **Mention the video in your subject line.** Subject lines like "Quick video for you, [Name]" or "Recorded this for [Company]" increase open rates.
- **Keep the email text brief.** One or two sentences of context, then the video thumbnail, then a short closing with a CTA. Do not write a long email around the video.
- **Place the video prominently.** Do not bury it below three paragraphs of text. The video should be the focal point of the email.
- **Include a text-based CTA below the video.** Not everyone will click the video, so provide an alternative path to engage.

## Embedding Video in LinkedIn Messages

LinkedIn supports video in messages, though the approach differs from email.

### Direct Upload

You can record and upload a video directly in a LinkedIn message. This is the simplest approach and keeps the prospect within the LinkedIn ecosystem. The video appears as a playable thumbnail in the message.

### Link to Hosted Video

Alternatively, paste a link to a video hosted on Loom, Vidyard, or a similar platform. Include a brief text message explaining what the video covers and why you made it for them.

### InMail with Video

If you are using LinkedIn InMail, including a video link can significantly improve response rates compared to text-only InMails. The video serves as a differentiator in a channel where most messages follow the same generic template.

## Real Outreach Sequence Example

Here is a complete five-touch outreach sequence that integrates video at strategic points.

### Touch 1: Personalized Video Email

Subject: Quick video for [Name] at [Company]

Body: "Hi [Name], I recorded a 60-second video specifically for you about [topic]. [Video thumbnail link]. Let me know if a quick chat would make sense."

### Touch 2: LinkedIn Connection Request (Day 3)

Message: "Hi [Name], I recently sent you a quick video about [topic]. Would love to connect here on LinkedIn as well. I share a lot of content about [relevant topic] that might be useful."

### Touch 3: Follow-Up Email with Value (Day 7)

Subject: Following up + something useful

Body: "Hi [Name], wanted to circle back on my video from last week. Also thought you might find this relevant -- [link to article, case study, or resource]. Happy to chat if the timing works."

### Touch 4: Short Follow-Up Video (Day 12)

Subject: 30-second follow-up, [Name]

Body: "Hi [Name], quick 30-second video to follow up. [Video thumbnail link]. No pressure either way."

### Touch 5: Testimonial Video Email (Day 18)

Subject: How [similar company] achieved [result]

Body: "Hi [Name], I wanted to share a quick clip from [client name] about how they [achieved result]. Thought it might resonate given [connection to their situation]. [Video thumbnail link]. Worth a conversation?"

## Measuring Video Outreach Performance

Track these metrics across your video outreach campaigns:

- **Video view rate:** Percentage of recipients who play the video
- **Watch-through rate:** Average percentage of the video watched
- **Reply rate:** Percentage of recipients who respond
- **Meeting booking rate:** Percentage of responses that convert to calls
- **A/B test results:** Compare sequences with video against text-only sequences

Most video outreach platforms provide these analytics automatically. Review them weekly and adjust your approach based on what the data shows.
